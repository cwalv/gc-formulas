Good question to sit with, because the right answer depends a lot on what "investing" means for you specifically — contributing upstream, building on top, hardening your own usage, or evaluating whether to keep betting on it at all. I'll assume you mean some mix of "what to push the project toward" and "what to validate before going deeper personally," and pull them apart where they diverge.

On whether anything's clearly better to invest in: from what I can see, no, with caveats. Intent is the closest spiritual cousin and has a more polished surface, but it's closed-source, Mac-only, paid, and the Coordinator-driven model is centrally directed in a way that's less interesting than gascity's pull-from-ready-frontier substrate model. LangGraph is more mature but lives one layer up — it's a framework you'd build *with*, not a substrate that holds your durable work-state across sessions. Temporal is industrial-strength but wrong-shaped for "agents working a graph of issues." The local-tier cohort (Conductor, Vibe Kanban, Claude Squad) is mostly thinner — parallel-agent runners without much workflow shape underneath. The one I'd actually watch as a potential alternative is whatever crystallizes around A2A + a durable-substrate-of-choice, because that's the standards-shaped path; but right now nothing in that shape has the application-level opinions gascity is forming, and standards-shaped ecosystems take years to fill in. So gascity is the most interesting bet in its specific shape, and the question is what to do to make the bet pay off rather than where to switch.

The single thing I'd push hardest on is **stabilizing the worker contract**. Not by writing an SDK that replaces the prompt — Yegge's principle on prompt-as-protocol is right, and I retracted the SDK overreach earlier — but by *crystallizing* the contract so the prompt gets shorter and more stable over time rather than longer and more churny. Right now graph-worker.md is 164 lines of bash recipes, and every new orchestrator primitive adds more. The end-state I'd aim for is a worker prompt closer to bd prime in size: maybe 30-50 lines, covering the four irreducible agent-side responsibilities (do the work, classify failures, escalate when stuck, self-report context exhaustion) and pointing at well-named commands for everything else. Getting from where it is to that end-state requires moving specific responsibilities into the runtime: continuation-group sibling pre-assignment into the controller, bead-kind routing filters into the queue layer, the three-queue startup search into a single `bd next` or equivalent command, the polling-before-drain logic into something like `bd wait`. None of that requires changing the architecture; it's all "move the bash from prompt to code."

The way to phrase this to the team or in your own head: *"every line of bash in graph-worker.md is a place where the protocol leaks; the goal is to make the protocol legible enough that the prompt describes intent rather than mechanics."* And the way to test progress on it is concrete and measurable: line count of graph-worker.md trending down over time, even as orchestrator features get added. If the protocol is genuinely crystallizing, prompt length should decouple from feature count. If it's not, every new feature still adds a section to the prompt and you've got an unbounded surface. That's a falsifiable bet you can track release-over-release.

The second focus area is **schema enforcement and finalizer wiring** — the gap mol-review-quorum exposed where formulas declare contracts but the runtime doesn't enforce them, and declare finalizers but they aren't called. The cost of this gap compounds: every workflow that ships with declared-but-unenforced schemas trains users to ignore the schema declarations, which devalues the whole contract layer. Closing the gap doesn't require a big architectural move; it requires `gc.output_json_schema` to actually validate at bead-close time and reject `outcome=pass` if validation fails, and it requires the formula compiler to wire declared finalizers to the appropriate step rather than falling back to agent execution. Both are bounded engineering tasks with clear acceptance criteria.

Phrase it as: *"formulas should fail loudly when contracts are violated, not silently degrade to LLM-best-effort."* Test it by picking three formulas with schema declarations, deliberately producing invalid outputs, and verifying the system rejects them; then deliberately producing valid outputs and verifying the finalizer actually runs as a deterministic Go function rather than an LLM call. If those tests pass, the schema layer is real; if they don't, the schema layer is documentation theater regardless of how much exists.

The third focus area is the **forward/reverse derivation symmetry** you brought up — and specifically, making reverse derivation a first-class pattern with at least three reference formulas (changelog, postmortem, doc-sync) shipped in the core pack. You already have changelog working personally; getting it into the substrate as a canonical example would prove out the pattern for the community. The reason this matters more than it might seem: reverse derivation is the answer to the human-granularity problem we talked through, and it's also the thing that makes the project legible to people who aren't already deep in it. A potential adopter who sees "this system can keep its own docs current and write its own changelog" understands the value proposition in seconds; one who sees "this system lets you compose retry loops over fan-out steps" needs ten minutes of context. The reverse-derivation story is the marketing story *and* the validation that the substrate is rich enough to project upward into human-readable artifacts.

Phrase it as: *"the substrate has to be rich enough to derive human-facing artifacts from, and we prove it by deriving them."* Test it by running the project's own changelog/docs through reverse-derivation formulas and judging output quality, then by running a postmortem formula on a real hard-failure chain and seeing whether the output is actually useful to humans. If the derived artifacts are good, the substrate is carrying the right structured state; if they're vague and unhelpful, the per-bead structured residue is too thin and you need to push more contract-richness into the worker outputs.

The fourth focus area is **eval coverage for the worker protocol** — which is the thing the prompt-as-protocol approach has been weakest on across the whole field, gascity included. If the worker contract is going to remain a prompt, then the prompt needs to be tested the way code gets tested: a corpus of (graph state, agent capability) → (expected protocol behavior) cases, run regularly against the actual frontier models, with regression alerts when a model update breaks compliance. This is the thing that turns "trust the LLM to follow instructions" from hope into engineering. Without it, every model upgrade is a roll of the dice; with it, the team gets early warning when, say, Claude 4.7 stops handling continuation groups correctly, or Gemini misinterprets the failure-class taxonomy. I'd actually argue this is the single capability that most legitimizes the Yegge principle in practice — "prompts improve as models improve" only holds if you can detect when they don't.

Phrase it as: *"if the protocol is a prompt, the prompt is code, and code needs tests."* Test progress by counting protocol-eval cases under CI and watching the pass rate per model. A mature state looks like: every documented worker behavior has at least one eval case, every supported model runs the full suite on every release, regressions block merges. That's a lot of investment but it's the thing that makes the Yegge bet defensible rather than aspirational.

If I had to rank these by leverage: schema enforcement (#2) and worker-protocol evals (#4) are the highest-leverage maturity moves because they make existing functionality *trustworthy* rather than adding new functionality. Worker-contract crystallization (#1) is the highest-leverage *clarity* move because it directly addresses the half-baked feeling you described — most of that feeling is probably coming from the worker protocol surface mutating under you. Reverse-derivation (#3) is the highest-leverage *adoption* move because it makes the project legible to outsiders and self-maintaining for insiders. If I were prioritizing for your specific situation — already using it, wanting it to mature into something you can rely on — I'd push #1 hardest, because it's the one that most directly determines whether using gascity feels like riding a moving train or standing on solid ground.

A general way to phrase the overall validation rubric, across all four: *"a year from now, has the surface area gotten more concrete or more diffuse?"* The honest test of whether gascity is maturing isn't feature count — it's whether the primitives stabilize and the prompt+contract surface stops shifting. If in a year graph-worker.md is shorter, the schema layer enforces real contracts, three reverse-derivation formulas ship in the core, and a protocol eval suite gates releases, the project has crossed from "interesting bet" to "load-bearing infrastructure." If any of those still aren't true, the bet didn't quite land and you should reassess whether the next year is likely to be different.

That MCP-vs-skills analogy is really sharp and I think it generalizes to something important about the whole agent-tooling design space.

The pattern is: there's a thing models can already do well in natural language, and then there's a structured interface that re-encodes the same thing in a typed/declarative form. The structured version *feels* more principled — it has a schema, it has parameters, it has validation, it composes. But the cost is that you've moved expressiveness from a medium the model is fluent in (prose) to a medium that requires translation back and forth (TOML, JSON schema, declarative DSL). And the translation isn't free — every formula author has to learn the formula DSL, every formula consumer (the runtime) has to interpret it, every change to what's expressible has to extend the DSL rather than just rewording the prompt. You've paid real costs to get something the model could already do better in prose.

The MCP vs skills version is the cleanest case of this. If you already have a CLI, wrapping it in MCP is mostly ceremony — you're describing the CLI in JSON Schema, the model is reading that schema, the model is producing JSON, MCP is unmarshalling JSON into CLI arguments. You've done a round-trip through a typed protocol for something the model could have done by reading the CLI's `--help` and constructing the right invocation directly. A skill that just says "to do X, run `tool subcommand --flag value`" is shorter, more flexible, easier to update, and survives the CLI changing under it. MCP earns its keep when the tool isn't already a CLI (network services, stateful sessions, capabilities discovery) — but for CLIs specifically, skills usually win.

Formulas have the same structural problem. A formula like mol-review-quorum encodes "run two reviewer lanes with these models, synthesize the results, here are the schemas" in TOML with templating, schema declarations, retry policies, finalizer wiring. The prose version is "review this change by getting independent assessments from two reviewer models and synthesizing them." The prose version is shorter, more adaptable to the specific situation ("but skip the security-focused one because this PR is docs-only"), and gets better as the model improves. The formula version is more reusable in a literal-template sense, but the reusable-template property might not be worth what you paid for it: the DSL surface, the runtime to interpret it, the maintenance of keeping formulas in sync with what models can actually do.

Your sketch of "create beads to accomplish these tasks: {{preflight}} {{build}}" is exactly the lightweight version, and it's probably most of what formulas should be. A skill that says "when you need to run this kind of workflow, here's the prompt template with these slots, fill it in and just do what it says." No runtime. No schema enforcement. No reconciler. The model reads the template, substitutes the slots, executes against beads using normal commands. You get reuse — the template is reusable, it's parameterized — without the infrastructure cost of a workflow engine. If the template needs to evolve, edit the markdown. If you need a new slot, add it. If the model gets better and needs less hand-holding, shorten the template. The expressiveness gradient is continuous and the cost of change is near-zero.

The "everything app" point I want to come back to because I think it's the broader pattern. WeChat-as-21st-century-interface is right and it's because *natural language plus structured feedback* is the universal interface for any task that involves cognition. You don't need a special app for each domain; you need an interface where you can describe intent, see what the system is doing, and provide structured nudges when it goes wrong. That's the affordance most "apps" actually wrap — they're just opinionated UIs over a small workflow. Once the cognition layer is general enough to handle the underlying task, the opinionated UI is mostly overhead. The interface collapses to "tell me what you want, here's what I'm doing, here's where I need direction."

What survives in that world isn't apps — it's the *artifacts* that domain expertise produces. Plans, specs, behavioral conventions, structured feedback channels, the dense input-side context we were just discussing. Those are the things humans bring that the cognition layer can't produce from nothing. Everything that's currently a "feature" of an app — a particular form, a particular workflow, a particular set of buttons — gets replaced by the cognition layer interpreting intent against the right context. The app dissolves into the artifacts that make it possible.

Formulas-as-prompt-templates fit cleanly into this picture: they're a way of curating reusable input-side artifacts. A formula isn't a runtime construct, it's *content* — a parameterized prompt that encodes a workflow shape the way a skill encodes how to use a tool. Same category. Same mechanism (natural language with slots). Same evolution model (edit the markdown). The formula DSL was solving a problem at the wrong layer; the actual problem is "how do I curate reusable agent-input content," and the answer to that is probably "markdown files with conventions," not "TOML with a runtime interpreter."

Which gives a clean overall design principle for the input-side tools we identified as the actual frontier: **prefer content over runtime**. Whatever you can express as a markdown template, skill, or AGENTS.md fragment, prefer that over a DSL with a runtime. The runtime is only earning its keep when the artifact genuinely can't be expressed in prose — durable execution semantics, exactly-once side effects, concurrent state mutations across processes. For agent-input artifacts specifically (plans, formulas, skills, conventions), content is almost always the right answer, because the consumer is fluent in prose and the cost of a runtime is high.

That's actually a pretty satisfying place for the conversation to land, because it ties together a lot of what we worked through. The architectural mistake repeated across the field is *trying to encode in runtime what should be content*. MCP-when-a-CLI-would-do. Formulas-when-prompt-templates-would-do. Reconcilers-when-the-model-can-orchestrate. Frameworks-when-prose-would-do. The model layer's improving fast enough that "express it as content for the model to read" beats "encode it as a runtime construct" almost every time, and the gap is widening. The work that actually pays back is curating excellent content — plans, skills, conventions, templates — and trusting the model to do the rest.

Yeah, that tension is real and I don't think it dissolves. Both things are true and they pull against each other.

The case for "prefer content over runtime" is strong in the abstract. The case for "but UIs and workflows have real value" is also strong, and it's not just nostalgia for the old way. A well-designed workflow UI communicates *more* than the equivalent prose because it makes structure visible. When you see a kanban board, you immediately perceive "these are the stages, this is the work-in-progress limit, this card is stuck." When you read prose describing the same state, you reconstruct that mental model serially. The UI is doing real cognitive work — chunking, spatializing, surfacing pattern at a glance. That's not overhead, it's bandwidth.

The deeper version of this: opinionated UIs and workflows are *compressed agreements about how to think about a domain*. A good issue tracker isn't just a database with forms; it's a position on what "priority" means, what "blocked" means, when a thing is "done." Those positions are valuable precisely because they constrain the conversation to productive shape. You can have a long unstructured chat about the state of a project, or you can look at the board and know in 5 seconds. The board encodes thousands of micro-decisions about how to represent project state. Those decisions are content too, just frozen into UI form.

So the honest framing isn't "content beats runtime" — it's that the tradeoff has shifted. There's a category of runtime that's actively bad now (runtimes that constrain model cognition, runtimes that re-encode in DSL what the model handles natively in prose, runtimes that pay for ambition that model improvement is erasing). And there's a category of runtime that's still earning its keep (the compressed-agreement kind that makes structure visible at a glance, the durable-execution kind that handles exactly-once, the substrate kind that provides agent-shared state). The mistake the field is making isn't building runtimes — it's building the wrong kind, ones that try to own what the model does best while neglecting what the model can't do alone.

The interesting design question your "unsatisfying" reaction points at is: **what would an opinionated UI for the agent-input frontier actually look like?** Not a UI that constrains the model's reasoning, but one that makes the *human's* work of curating input-side artifacts more legible and faster. Imagine an interface for plan authoring that surfaces "your spec covers 80% of the workflows, here are the 20% the model would have to improvise" — that's a workflow with real value, encoding agreements about what makes a spec complete enough. Or an interface for skill libraries that shows "this skill was used 47 times this week, success rate dropped after Claude 4.7, here's the diff in skill output that suggests why" — again, opinionated UI doing real cognitive work, not constraining the model. Those are bandwidth wins.

The pattern: opinionated UI is still valuable where it compresses agreements *about the work humans are doing*, not where it constrains *the work the model is doing*. Issue trackers, planning tools, skill curation tools, eval dashboards, agent-fleet operations cockpits — those are all places where a well-designed UI beats prose because the user is a human trying to manage scale or complexity that prose flatten. The category that's losing value is the workflow-runtime-that-constrains-the-model category: formula DSLs, declarative agent compositions, prompt-as-cage frameworks. Different beasts, same word "workflow," different fates.

This actually loops back to something we already noticed but didn't quite finish thinking through: the granularity-mismatch problem. Agents want dense input-side artifacts; humans want rollups. That's a workflow problem in the *good* sense — there's real value in tooling that lets humans operate at their natural granularity while agents operate at theirs, and a UI mediating between the two views earns its keep. The kanban-board-over-bead-graph thing Intent is doing is genuinely useful work; the formula-engine-over-bead-graph thing canonical beads is doing is less so. Same substrate, but one of those UIs is helping a human while the other is constraining a model.

So maybe the more useful framing is: **opinionated UI for humans, content for agents.** Where the artifact is consumed by a human, opinionated UI is great because humans benefit from compression and structure. Where the artifact is consumed by a model, prefer content because the model is more fluent in prose than any DSL. The boundary line is "who's reading this thing." Plans go to agents → content (markdown). Plan-authoring tooling is for humans → opinionated UI makes sense. Beads themselves are consumed by agents → content-heavy (long descriptions, embedded context). Bead-rollup views are for humans → opinionated UI (kanban, dashboards, dependency graphs visualized). Formulas-as-runtime were getting this backwards — encoding agent-facing content as DSL that needed a runtime to interpret, paying runtime costs for what the model could read as prose.

The unsatisfying-but-true version of the answer: there's real value in clearer workflows, but the value lives on the human side of the human-agent boundary, and the architectural mistake repeated across the field is putting it on the agent side. The right design uses UI affordances where humans are working and prose where agents are working, and gets the boundary right.

And one more honest piece: even with all this, there's a real possibility we just haven't found the right *runtime abstractions* for the agent side yet. The current crop of agent-facing runtimes look bad because they're encoding the wrong things, but that doesn't mean there's no good runtime abstraction. Maybe something more like a substrate for *agent self-organization* — primitives that help agents coordinate among themselves without telling them how — would earn its keep in a way that current formula engines don't. Hard to say. The space is young, and "content beats runtime today" doesn't necessarily mean "content always beats runtime." It just means today's runtimes are mostly aimed at the wrong target, and the obvious move is content while we figure out what the right runtime target would actually be.
